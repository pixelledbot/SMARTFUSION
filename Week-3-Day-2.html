K-means Clustering Algorithm - Introduction

K-means is a popular unsupervised learning algorithm used for clustering similar data points into groups or "clusters." Unlike supervised learning, it doesn't require labeled data. It works by grouping data into K clusters based on feature similarity, with each data point belonging to the cluster with the nearest mean (centroid).

Working of K-means

The K-means algorithm follows these main steps:

  • Choose the number of clusters K.
  • Initialize K centroids randomly from the dataset.
  • Assign each data point to the nearest centroid based on distance (usually Euclidean).
  • Recalculate centroids as the mean of all points in the cluster.
  • Repeat assignment and update steps until centroids stabilize or maximum iterations are reached.

This process minimizes intra-cluster distance and maximizes inter-cluster separation.

Choosing the Value of K

Determining the optimal number of clusters K is crucial for meaningful clustering. A common method is the Elbow Method:

  • Run K-means for a range of K values.
  • Calculate the Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS) for each K.
  • Plot K vs WCSS and look for an "elbow" point where the rate of WCSS decrease slows down.

This elbow point is considered the optimal K, balancing accuracy and simplicity.

K-means Clustering - Example

Suppose we have a dataset of students with features like study hours and test scores. K-means can group them into performance-based clusters (e.g., high performers, average, low performers).

After applying K-means with K=3, we might get three clusters representing:

  • Cluster 1: High scores, high study hours
  • Cluster 2: Moderate scores and study time
  • Cluster 3: Low scores, low study hours

Each cluster helps educators understand student behavior and target specific interventions.
